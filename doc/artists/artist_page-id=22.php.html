Goran Bregovic - Artist Page
Goran Bregovic
Discography  ·  Booking Agency
Biography:
 

Born in Sarajevo of a Serbian mother and a Croatian father, he began his music studies in the city conservatory, where he studied violin without much enthusiasm. Goran founded his first band at the age of sixteen, and he became a habitual character in the cultural circles of the Bosnian capital. There, in the mid 1970s, he met Emir Kusturica, who was beginning to work as an amateurs film director and played in a punk rock band. 

The bonds that united Goran Bregovic and Emir Kusturica were not the result of whim nor an arrangement of circumstances. In addition to their professional collaboration, crowned by the success of the movie Time of the Gypsies, Arizona Dream and Underground, a true history of friendship exists. They have shared their first steps, the first time they got drunk, and mainly the same immoderate love for rock music. "Rock music occupied a capital place in our life at that time. It was the only possibility that we had of expressing our dissatisfaction openly and publically without running the risk of being arrested and sent to the slammer (jail)." 

After the protest stage, it was time to return to school. Emir went to Czechoslovakia to study at the prestigious cinema school in Prague. Goran continued his philosophy and sociology studies that, without a doubt, would have led him to a teaching job, if it hadn't been for the enormous success of his first album. He continued for fifteen years with his band Bijelo Dugme (The White Button), during which, between endless tours, Goran became the idol of the country's youth.

Goran was liberated from this troublesome role at the end of the 1980s when he started to compose the original soundtrack for the Time of the Gypsies movie, and fulfilled his childhood dream to have a small house by the Adriatic coast. But the dream didn't last very long as war exploded in Yugoslavia, and Goran was forced to abandon everything, settling in Paris, where he encountered again his old friend, Emir. Goran, himself of mixed origin (Serbian-Croatian), married a Bosnian woman. 

Coming from the same background, the same generation, wrapped in the same problems, Goran Bregovic and Emir Kusturica formed a team where complicity is such that they don't need words in order to understand each other. In the same way that Nino Rota worked with Fellini or Michael Nyman with Peter Greenaway, Goran had absolute freedom to compose the original soundtrack for Arizona Dream. 
The result is in the measure of the movie, lyrical, innovative and particularly surprising. An intuitive composer, Goran has left voluntarily in the backroom all the illusions of sophistication and perfectionism that usually banish film music to the range of simple sound illustrations. His compositions have borrowed, on the contrary, the wildest and liveliest of the lesser-known folklore of the Balkans. "One of the big successes of Emir's movies are that he shows life as it is, that is, full of holes, indecisions, the unexpected. It is their imperfect and disorderly side, that I wanted to preserve at any cost. Even the songs recorded by Iggy Pop have had an effect. It's just his backed by an orchestra of old Gypsies that blow their old trumpets and play cow horns. It is truly very simple." 

What Goran doesn't say is that it is without a doubt one of the better works by Iggy Pop in the last ten years. What he also forgets to mention is that ultimate simplicity only belongs to those people of great talent. 

Patrice Chéreau entrusted Goran with the soundtrack for Queen Margot, Golden Palm in 1994 at Cannes Film Festival. Goran composed a majestic soundtrack with rock accents. The music for Kusturica's latest movie, Underground, Golden Palm 1995 in Cannes, was also carried out by Goran. 

After he quit making rock music in 1985, Goran's music was not performed live. A series of concerts in Greece and Sweden in the summer of 1995, in which Goran played with a group of ten traditional musicians, combined with a 50 person choir and a symphony orchestra, changed this situation. He played "music for movies" live again in Greece, Poland, Switzerland, Turkey and Yugoslavia. The number of tickets sold in these concerts (between 3,500 and 10.000) confirm the influence of his music. 

In May of 1998 Goran Bregovic began a tour through Portugal, France and Italy, that led him to perform a single concert in Spain that year, at the Pirineos Sur (South Pyrenees) festival, where he was unanimously recognized as the best act programmed that year. During April of 1999 he carried out a tour through several Spanish cities (Zaragoza, Gijón, Madrid and Murcia), that harvested unsurpassable reviews among the daily press and the specialized media, that consecrated Goran Bregovic as one of the most important world music artists of our time. In September of that same year he participated in the 19th International Jazz and Flamenco Seminar organized by the Taller de Musics which ended with a master class in front a large audience at the Barcelona Cathedral Square.

Bregovic's soundtracks include Time of the Gypsies (Kuduz, Kamarad -PolyGram - Komuna, 1991); Arizona Dream (Kamarad - PolyGram - Komuna, 1993);
Muzica za filmove ( PGP RTS, 1994); La reine Margot (Kamarad - PolyGram - Komuna), 1994; Underground (Kamarad - PolyGram, 1995);
P.S. (Komuna, 1996); Ederlezi (PolyGram, 1998).

His recording collaborations include the following albums: Alkistis Protopsalti, Paralekstika (Polydor, 1991); W.AA., Silence of the Balkans, (project for Thessalonica, European Cultural Capital), 1997; Sezen Aksu, Dugun Ve Cenaze, (PolyGram France, 1997); Giorgio Dalaras, Thessaloniki - Yannena Me Dio Papoutsia Panina (Minos -Emi, 1997).

Bijelo Dugme was the group led by Goran Bregovic between 1974 and 1985. It released a total of 14 recordings for the Jugoton and Kamarad labels. The latest ones were:
Mramor, Kamen i zeljezo (Kamarad-Diskoton, 1987) and Ciribiribela (Kamarad-Komuna-Diskoton, 1988).
