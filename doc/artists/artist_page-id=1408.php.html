Bisayah Gong Orchestra - Artist Page
Bisayah Gong Orchestra
Similar Music
Biography:
 

Gongs, especially vertically hung ones, used to be found in every village in Sarawak (Malaysia), where they were highly valued for their religious and economic value. They were played at festivals, occasional healing ceremonies, receptions for important visitors, used as signals of danger or a request for help when a person is about to die or at childbirth. Various villages own the individual gongs and derive their status from this ownership. When a person dies, the gongs that belong to his family may not be played during the mourning period.

The music played by the Gong Orchestra is not very well known outside the Bisaya villages or kampungs which are mainly found in Limbang. A full orchestra would consist of the dumbak, bebandil, agong, tawak and gulintangan.

The dumbak is a drum made from a hollowed out log and covered at one end with the skin of a monitor lizard.

There are 3 types of vertically hung brass gongs. The first is a set of 3 medium sized, single bossed bebandil in 2 types ? 1 taritik and 2 bandil. These are usually handled by 2 players and is the equivalent of the rhythm guitar in a modern day band. They are usually decorated with dragons and geometrical designs. The second is the agong which are wide but shallow-rimmed gongs. These are the largest and deepest sounding of the gongs. Third is the tawak which ?talk? to each other. The tawak anak produces the ngembua or ?question?, while the tawak indu ?replies? with the peningkul sound. These have a large and prominent boss and is struck with padded beaters usually made of rubber.

Then there are the Gulintang which has 8-9 small gongs laid out on a wooden frame and this forms the melodic or ?lead guitar? role in the orchestra. The tunes or gandang played by the orchestra would usually depend on the occasion ? a death, perhaps, or a marriage, or an emergency or during the Temarok or harvest festival.

From the Bidayuh community comes the wooden xylophones or the gulintang which consists of 10-11 slats of wood. These are hit with wooden beaters and can be used as single or double-line melodic instruments.

The bamboo flutes are principally played by the Bidayuh for entertainment (though not for dancing). Some of the 5-hole mouth flutes are used in rituals to recall the spirit of people who have fainted or in a trance. Smaller flutes are played by young men as a preliminary to courting.

Junggotan or jew?s harps are used for both entertainment and courting. Usually, they are played in faster tempi or speeds than the flutes and their rhythms are more strongly accented.

The perunchong is a bamboo ensemble which is a set of tuned bamboo rods which are beaten to imitate the music of gongs. Usually these are played by people returning home after planting padi.

The group also represents the Ibans in the form of the sape, gendang kantu and the serunai which is a one stringed fiddle with a bow. It is made from bamboo and wood and animal skin.

A lot of sape music is performed by the Orang Ulu musicians. In the past few years, this thumb-plucked lute and its characteristic strongly accented melodies have traveled a long way away from Sarawak. The sape is the most popular instrument among the various Orang Ulu groups. It is usually made of soft wood such as the jelutong or gita or adau. Normally this is played by the men, either as a solo instrument or as a duet while men or women dance.

The ensemble also performs Penan sinui songs, which are sung from the throat. Sinui are popular tunes sung in the Belaga & Silat Rivers and in the Baram region. Usually, they are sung without instruments and the main singer sings an impromptu narrative in poetic rhyme, which is accompanied by a form of ?choral harmony?. Sinui often express feelings of love, happiness, merriment, sadness, loneliness or grief. The beauty of the sentiment expressed in the rhyme and narrative is often equally matched in the vocal style.

And there are also the incredibly difficult to play selingut (nose flutes) which are used to convey love messages by both men and women.
