Samia - Artist Page
Samia

Biography:
 

Samia Farah was born on August 2nd, 1971, in Tunisia. As in other years, her father, bursar at the University Village of Paris, and her mother, a nurse, were spending their holidays in Tunisia. The couple have been resident in France since the seventies, but their families still live in Tunisia and Samia has often returned there.

Like many fiercely independent people, Samia does not think that her personality can be grasped by studying the events that have marked her past. At the age of 16, she decided to take a break from her studies to spend a few months on the other side of the Mediterranean, in Tunisia, a temporary refuge for a teenager in search of an identity. There, among other things, she discovered reggae.

Her father had always been a music lover, listening freely and extravagantly to everything from jazz to European and Arab classics, albeit with a certain preference for the Egyptian crooner Muhammad 'Abd al Wallhâb, known as the supreme modernizer, the first artist to have proved that Western and Eastern culture are compatible. So Samia's ear traveled freely from one world to another. Back in France, Sarah Vaughan and Nina Simone caught Samia's attention and gave her an idea of what is meant by vocal style. Then came rap and ragga: she experimented with their broken phrasing in the female group Mama Benz.

Gradually, she developed her own style, somewhere between the velvet timbre of jazz singers and the staggered flow of hip-hop, a style that doesn't force me to yell, that lets me say my stuff calmly. By stuff, she means her lyrics, enumerations of the inevitable bruises and ulcers that a sensitivity such as hers ultimately suffers in a world such as ours. She says that her words do not match her voice and her originality and charm spring from this contradiction. If we have a clear impression of uncertain hedonism in Cool, the opening song on her first album, this is due to her refined ability to blend the bitter and the sweet, vocally suggesting laziness, but using words to eliminate any hint of inanity or rapture.

Samia excels in her role as a thief of happiness: her singing is a caress and her tongue a spur. Gratingly sensual, cynically inflexible, fatalistically pugnacious, she finally allows us a glimpse of the talent that governs all her other gifts: femininity. Samia does not dare write about love because my words don't measure up to what I feel. Set to music with the help of Giovanny de la Hunda, her first album generally breaks with past forms in the albeit highly-crowded world of French song.

As if freed of the weight of fashion and impervious to those attempts at intimidation that schools specialize in, Samia sings jazz, soul and reggae in her own way. Her spirit is brightened by the slight aesthetic mutiny that singles her out and leads firmly to subservience of form for the benefit of the performer. Like the wax crayons she uses to draw and the papier-mâché she sculpts with, Samia subjugates matter and wills. When she contacted the cult producer Adrian Sherwood, he agreed to work with her on 4 titles. Laurent de Wilde, André Ceccarelli and other top names in jazz also lent a hand. At her request, Adrian Boot (Bob Marley's photographer) took the photos for her album, while she handled the graphic design herself.
