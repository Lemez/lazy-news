Railroad Earth - Artist Page
Railroad Earth
Discography  ·  Similar Music
Biography:
 

In 2001 Railroad Earth formed out of live sessions at the Pocono Bluegrass Society open mics. A few weeks later they recorded their first demos and were invited to play Telluride, Grey Fox and High Sierra music festivals before they ever played a formal gig.

As the frontman for popular Jersey roots act From Good Homes, Todd Sheaffer covered a lot of ground in the years before Railroad Earth. It was a time when Sheaffer, mandolinist John Skehan and bluegrass champions Carbone and Goessling were in between projects that the band members first came together. While they knew of each other from the Garden State?s vibrant roots scene, they hadn?t played together before the fall of 2000. It was during a series of open-mic events, sponsored by the Pocono Bluegrass Society, that the initial four first began playing together.

Bandmates Tim Carbone (violin, accordion, guitar) and Andy Goessling (banjo, dobro, guitar, etc.) literally traveled more than a million miles going from gig to gig in their previous band, Blue Sparks From Hell.

Sheaffer had written some new songs and played them for Carbone, Goessling and Skehan, who helped to adapt them into neo-bluegrass numbers. Within a couple of months, the Railroad Earth line-up was rounded out by the addition of (drummer) Carey Harmon (a former member of The Hour and the Bobby Syvarth Band) and finally, upright bassist, Johnny Grubb.

The band is an amalgam of influences and experiences. Carbone and Goessling have bluegrass backgrounds, while Sheaffer is a singer/songwriter schooled on such icons as Dylan, the Dead, the Stones and Neil Young. With roots in the piano, Skehan plays the mandolin as if his fingers were dancing on the ivories. The group also brings together players of different ages and perspectives. ?It?s sort of a multi-generational band, so there are influences that come from different moments in time,? says Sheaffer. ?It?s those different energies coming together that have created our sound.?

The band?s debut, The Black Bear Sessions, arrived in 2001 (BOS Music), following strong internet exposure. That was a year that found Railroad Earth winning the hearts of many a roots music fan as its improvisational style and bluegrass influence recalled the likes of the Grateful Dead and New Grass Revival, despite the fact that Sheaffer sounds more like Crowded House?s Neil Finn. It was their blazing instrumental work and instantly classic songs that really connected with fans.

Their Sugar Hill Records debut, Bird in a House, followed in 2002, thrilling critics and fans alike. While initially the band had begun to build a reputation with soulful sets at the country?s biggest bluegrass festivals and small venues, Bird in a House accurately reflected the band?s potential and varied influences, going from bluegrass to folk to Celtic to rock. At the same time, it found Railroad Earth emerging as a songwriting force.

There?s a line in the song ?Long Way to Go? where Sheaffer talks about the experiences he and his current and former bandmates have had sharing the stage with such heroes as Bob Dylan, Bob Weir (of the Grateful Dead), Emmylou Harris and Ralph Stanley. ?When you stand up on the stage with them, you realize how magnificent they are and how far you still have to go, in terms of learning your craft.?

If Sheaffer and the other members of Railroad Earth still had a lot of ground to cover when he wrote ?Long Way to Go,? The Good Life cuts that distance down considerably.

When it came time to record The Good Life, the members of Railroad Earth felt it was time for something new. Since forming in 2001, the band had released two lauded albums that were largely documents of the Railroad Earth live experience. Both rootsy sets were full of songs that initially came to life onstage and were then crafted for the studio. The Good Life, however, is the product of a very different studio experience.

?This was the first record where we had even less of an idea of what might happen before we went into the studio,? says singer/songwriter/guitarist Todd Sheaffer. ?We weren?t drawing from any material that we?d ever played live. This record was all fresh material, written, arranged and recorded in the studio.?

The band?s previous disc, the critically acclaimed 2002 album Bird in a House, was more of an opportunity to showcase the group?s mighty chops, while preserving the strength and purity of their classic-sounding songs. On The Good Life, Railroad Earth?with the help of producer Stewart Lerman (Loudon Wainwright III, Dar Williams)?focused more on serving the song in its purest form. Instead of recording an album of songs they?d initially performed live, these tracks were snapshots of the moment of their creation, arranged specifically to serve the studio experience. ?We wanted to up the ante sonically and make a great record, rather than just a document of what we do?a document of the live band,? says Sheaffer.

The disc is still very much a Railroad Earth experience, lush with rich mandolins, acoustic guitars, violin, banjo, upright bass and many other musical surprises. Yet, there are clearly some more poppy moments throughout. The soulful, strangely tuned ?Said What You Mean,? for example, could be the band?s catchiest track yet. ?This lovin? is free/Come dream it with me/We?ll make it our home,? Sheaffer sings in the chorus.

?It passed the test at home,? he says with a laugh. ?That song has a sweetness and a soulfulness to it that I thought would be a real nice addition to the record. And [drummer] Carey [Harmon] laid down a beautiful deep, good ole soul pocket on that, which got our producer and engineer pretty excited.?

The lyrics on The Good Life run from the sweet and nostalgic to the slyly political. On the spirited sing-along ?Way of the Buffalo,? Sheaffer sings about our diminishing freedoms and the destruction of our individualism, due, in part, to the consolidation of small retail stores into mega-chains. But it?s all done with a sense of dark humor. ?Does your street look like mine?? he sings, as if he finds the whole thing strangely funny.

On the title track, ?The Good Life,? Sheaffer spins a utopian tale about moving from the city to a more simple life in the country. ?They built a little house out of stone they found. Put their backs to the plow?put their seeds in the ground?? It?s a story of love, life and simplicity.

The album-opening ?Storms? was initially inspired by a period during the fall of 2003 when Northwest New Jersey was being pummeled with intense rainfall, sparking the disc?s first lines, ?Soft thunder from across the meadow/Rain buckets on the kitchen floor.? ?The words started to take on some deeper significance in my life, and the lyrics began to expand to incorporate my own experiences over the past couple of years,? Sheaffer says.

Todd Sheaffer (vocals, guitar)

John Skehan (mandolin, bouzouki, piano, Hammond organ)

Tim Carbone (violin, viola, piano, accordion, toy piano, harmonium, electric guitar, kaen, backing vocals)

Carey Harmon (drums, percussion, backing vocals)

Andy Goessling (banjo, dobro, guitar, mandolin, ukulele, penny whistle, bouzouki, sax. flute, marxophone)

Johnny Grubb (bass)
