Nashville Bluegrass Band - Artist Page
Nashville Bluegrass Band
Discography
Biography:
 
 Alan O?Bryant, guitarist Pat Enright, mandolinist Mike Compton and bass player Mark Hembree originally formed the group in 1984 as the backing band for a Minnie Pearl tour. The debut Nashville Bluegrass Band album appeared a year later. Fiddler Stuart Duncan joined in time for the group?s 1986 LP. This edition of the group lasted until 1990, when Compton decided to retire from music and Hembree also departed. Their replacements were Roland White and Gene Libbea.

The O?Bryant/Enright/Duncan/White/Libbea configuration of The Nashville Bluegrass Band won Grammy Awards for its recordings of 1993 and 1995, made videos that were programmed on CMT, won four Vocal Group of the Year awards from the International Bluegrass Music Association and twice earned the IBMA?s top honor, Entertainer of the Year. The NBB was bluegrass music?s ?it? group for more than a decade.

In late 2000, White decided to retire from the road to concentrate on teaching and Libbea quit to move to Colorado. Original mandolinist Compton had worked at a ski lodge in the Catskill Mountains, but was miserable away from music. He?d returned to Nashville, so O?Bryant called him to ask if he?d rejoin. Bass player Dennis Crouch had been tutored by original NBB bassist Hembree. When he heard that Libbea was leaving, he called O?Bryant expressing interest in the job.

The reconfigured Nashville Bluegrass Band made headlines immediately. In the spring of 2001 the group took on the challenge of performing with The Nashville Chamber Orchestra. Classical violinist Conni Elisor had composed ?Whiskey Before Breakfast: Partitas for Chamber Orchestra and Bluegrass Band.? The collaboration was performed at three concerts in Nashville and taped for broadcast on National Public Radio. In 2002 the new NBB lineup made its recording debut performing ?I Know What it Means to Be Lonesome? on The Nitty Gritty Dirt Band?s all-star album Will the Circle Be Unbroken Vol. 3.

By then, the group was already in the midst of the biggest concert tour of its career. Members Pat Enright, Stuart Duncan and Mike Compton had become part of The Soggy Bottom Boys for the O Brother Where Art Thou soundtrack phenomenon of 2001. The highly successful series of ?Down from the Mountain? tours that resulted in 2002-03 co-starred the Nashville Bluegrass Band with a who?s-who of acoustic music. In the wake of the Cold Mountain soundtrack, The Nashville Bluegrass Band joined the similar ?Great High Mountain? tour of early 2004.

?We got sidelined by so many things,? comments O?Bryant about the recording delay. ?Mike and Dennis both came on board in January 2001, and we wanted some time to incorporate them and get everyone to meld together. That took awhile.?

Personal problems contributed as well. During the recording hiatus, O?Bryant lost his wife Kathy following her long battle with cancer. Stuart Duncan?s father died unexpectedly during one of the ?Down From the Mountain? tours. O?Bryant was beset by vocal problems, leading the band to shift harmony parts and adjust its sound.

All five Nashville Bluegrass Band members have earned individual acclaim. Stuart Duncan has seven IBMA awards as Fiddler of the Year (1990-96). He is also a ?first call? session musician who plays on the albums of dozens of country superstars. Similarly, Dennis Crouch works on Music Row recording sessions whenever upright bass is called for. He is also the substitute bass player in the Grand Ole Opry staff band.

Alan O?Bryant?s song ?Those Memories of You? became a No. 1 hit for Linda Ronstadt, Dolly Parton and Emmylou Harris. He is also in demand as a bluegrass record producer whose recent projects include CDs by Junior Barber, Brittany Bailey and Valerie Smith. Mike Compton is on screen throughout the Down From the Mountain concert film as a mainstay in the ?house band.? Pat Enright provided the yodeling voice of actor John Turturro in O Brother and is a bluegrass songwriter.

Even when not working on its own albums, The Nashville Bluegrass Band has been in the studio with a wide variety of admirers. The group collaborated with Johnny Cash on the 1996 movie soundtrack Dead Man Walking. It has backed artists as diverse as Bernadette Peters and Clint Black in the studio. The eclectic Peter Rowan, Irish singer Maura O?Connell and the legendary black gospel group The Fairfield Four have all recorded with The Nashville Bluegrass Band.

The group has shared the stage at Carnegie Hall with Lyle Lovett and Mary Chapin Carpenter. That?s just one of its many live-performance highlights. The Nashville Bluegrass Band was the first bluegrass group to perform in the People?s Republic of China. It has also staged concerts in Egypt, Brazil, Crete, Bangladesh, Bahrain, Qatar, The Azores, Iraq and Israel, not to mention Denmark, Germany, France, Ireland, Switzerland, Turkey, Japan, Italy, Spain and England.

Throughout its history, the group has been viewed as being on bluegrass music?s youthful cutting edge. So it comes as something of a shock to know that The Nashville Bluegrass Band has been around for 20 years.

?I look around at these guys every once in awhile and realize that and, yeah, it is kind of a shocker,? says O?Bryant. ?But it?s a good feeling to have been around this long. We never felt like money or any kind of financial success was our big reward. We were just out to do what we wanted to do, musically. And we got to do that. We?re still getting to do that. This band is based on mutual respect. This band exists for the people in it and not the other way around."

Just in time to celebrate the group?s 20th anniversary came Twenty Year Blues, an album that marked a new creative high point for the Grammy Award winning quintet. ?This is what we were meant to sound like,? sayd the band?s Alan O?Bryant.

The secret, he said, was working at home. The project began taking shape in 2001-02 at various studios in Nashville. But O?Bryant had been having success as a producer by working with artists in his living room. Instead of worrying about a budget or trying to fit the recording into a rented block of studio time on Music Row, he suggested a new experiment for The Nashville Bluegrass Band.

?I said, ?I?m dying to try this at the house. If you guys want to come out there and experiment with an open mind, let?s give it a shot. If we get something, great. If we don?t, great. I?m cool.? Well, we came in there. I don?t know if it was just the comfort level or what, but what we got was better than what we?d done anyplace else. We wound up re-recording everything that we?d done earlier.

?It?s real comfortable out at my house. I?ve got a big room with a picture window that looks out over the woods in the hollow. We?d get out there and just wail away all day. We?d relax. We?d break to eat when we wanted to. Everybody was involved in the production, putting the material together and the arranging. We were able to polish the performances until we got them exactly like we wanted them.

?We?ve always been about the ?feel.? And that was what was so great about being able to make a record this way. We could play until we got the ?feel good? cut. ?

On Twenty Year Blues, The Nashville Bluegrass Band ?feels good? on instrumentals like ?Garfield?s Blackberry Blossom,? ?Pretty Red Lips? and ?Crossing the Cumberlands.? It updates old-time music like ?Gambling Barroom Blues,? ?Sitting on Top of the World? and ?Travelin? Railroad Man Blues.? The group showcases its trademark a cappella harmony-singing skills on ?Hush (Somebody?s Callin? My Name)? and recalls the legacies of Bill Monroe and John Hartford with a loping treatment of ?Old Riverman.?
