Heritage - Artist Page
Heritage
Discography
Biography:
 

Like the traditional music it celebrates and reinvents in ways both unexpected and inevitable, Heritage takes what it needs from the rich and varied terrain of the American musical landscape. From this source it delivers a respectful but unabashedly contemporary musical collection that ranges from the Appalachian gospel to African American chant.

"All these traditional songs come to us so brilliantly constructed and strong as purely musical entities," says Heritage producer Darol Anger, "that they can take just about any kind of musical setting and not lose their meaning and identity." Anger has interpreted the tunes through the prism of his own wide-ranging experience. It's perhaps inevitable that in Heritage he should so thoroughly recreate these familiar tunes.

Anger's career as a violinist, producer, arranger and composer has been a steady series of reinventions. Coming up in the eighties in the David Grisman Quintet, he helped create a new genre of string band music. The Montreux Band followed, becoming a musical model for the New Adult Contemporary format. Anger's jazz-oriented Turtle Island String Quartet redefined the traditional quartet and introduced the chamber music audience to American Vernacular rhythms and styles. His rule-breaking bluegrass-derived Psychograss brings him part way home to the tradition represented by Heritage.

 "People who do traditional music are fortunate," Anger says. "It's like having a strong family to depend on." With Heritage he depends heavily upon an extended family of musicians and singers. Mary Chapin Carpenter contributes a reading of "Pretty Polly" worthy of the greatest dramatic actress, with what amounts to a folk chamber orchestra backing her, complete with overture. In "Roving on a Winter's Night," John Gorka and Dar Williams, two of our most talented singer-songwriters, let us in on an intimate moment. A large group of musicians maintains that intimacy Bela Fleck, David Lindley, Victor Wooten, John Jennings and Darol among them, in a brilliant display of group counterpoint.

Edgar Meyer plays an extended, hair-raising solo on the ancient gospel hymn "Talk about Suffering," which also plays a string quartet off' against an unconventional ensemble of mandolin, cittern, harp and hammer dulcimer.

Instruments we've never heard in this context fit surprisingly well. Paul McCandless' bass clarinet gets to rip into the murder ballad "Willow Garden." steel pans participate in "Shenandoah."

Willie Nelson's raw, back porch rendition of Stephen Foster's "Hard Times" is complemented by Darol's equally raw fiddle and David Grisman' S urbane mandolin, supported by Phil Aaberg's all-encompassing piano. "Playing with Willie in the studio was like going onto another plane of consciousness Anger says. "Everybody was so happy to be there."

Mavis Staples and David Lindley join forces for a bone-chilling rendition of "0h Death," which is followed by a fiddle player's idea of heaven - an all fiddle version of Golden Slippers with a who's who of American Fiddle. Anger, Vassar Clements, John Hartford, Stuart Duncan, Matt Glaser, Sam Bush, and Tim O'Brien call themselves the Nashville Lumberyard, "because of all the sawing going on," says Hartford.

"La Ville Des Manteaux" features Anger and Cajun fiddle king Michael Doucet (Beausoleil) in an animated fiddle conversation backed by a burning crew including Mike Marshall, Phil Aaberg and the redoubtable Flecktone Victor Wooten.

The voice of Jane Siberry shapes both the overture and finale, opening the album with Anger's arrangement of Shenandoah and closing it with her own version containing new lyrics rewritten from an archetypal feminine perspective. Angers says, "Jane's concept is very deep. She deals so acutely with the realm of dream."

Anger provides the stitching in this American quilt. His arrangements and performances define the songs, drawing on his extensive experience as a folk and jazz performer. He has produced dozens of critically lauded recordings since 1977. He holds the String Chair of the International Association of Jazz Educators, and teaches in settings as seemingly unconnected as Mark O'Connor's Fiddle Camp and the Bremen, Germany Music Conservatory. His books of jazz and American traditional-style compositions for fiddle, string quartet and orchestra are studied by string students as far away as Australia and Singapore.

Anger was the recipient of a 1995 California Arts Council Composer Fellowship, and was a nominee for the 1997 CalArts Alpert Award. He has been a featured soloist on a number of motion picture soundtracks, and he wrote and performed the score for the Sundance Award-winning film "Best Offer."

Like the American quilt makers, Anger and the other artists on Heritage take a still vital tradition and reassemble it, adding new concepts and sometimes startling combinations of colors and ideas. Heritage is that most treasured of musical offerings, intensely and intimately familiar and at the same time thoroughly new.
