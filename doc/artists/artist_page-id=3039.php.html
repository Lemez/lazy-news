Regis Gizavo - Artist Page
Regis Gizavo
Booking Agency  ·  Similar Music
Biography:
 
Regis Gizavo, an accordionist from Tulear, presents himself not only as a defender of the traditions of his region (where the ethnic groups, Vezo, Masikoro and Mahafaly, co-exist), but also of modern, original music, absorbing like a sponge diverse influences with perfect ease. His experience in the country (immersion from his early childhood in trance music, performance of popular music with various variety groups, and pure research in collaboration with the guitarist D?Gary) have all gone to make him an accomplished musician. In 1990, was awarded Radio France International's "Discovery Prize".

Ever since his arrival in Europe in 1990, he's been seen by the side of musicians of every tendency: jazz, Middle Eastern, African, variety, etc. It was thanks to the Corsican group, I Muvrini with whom he's been performing since 1993, that his name became a household word. But it is in his own concerts and albums, as a duet with the percussionist, David Mirandon, that he expresses his true identity: that of a musician with his ears open and his heart irremediably Malagasy.

R?gis was barely six years old when he played at school parties with his father's diatonic accordion, the most common in Madagascar. When Regis was twelve, his father acquired a chromatic accordion with piano keys, on which the son learned immediately. With this instrument, he created a varied repertory for himself at once traditional and modern. By then, his interpretation of tradition was off the beaten track. He played on the chromatic accordion music made for the diatonic.

When at age nineteen, he began a professional career, it was with an old friend?s chromatic accordion with buttons. Again,  a new technique, a new learning experience. During his journey across Madagascar, he performed in each town in rented instruments, all kinds of accordions as well as valihas and guitars. As for his voice, it had an original quality, both vibrant and velvety, which did not go unnoticed neither by the jury of Radio France International (RFI) nor the leaders of I Muvrini. How to marry all of this talents? For this first album, Regis made a minimalist choice, that of duet with the percussionist David Mirandon. "We both made steps toward each other, I didn't impose the Malagasy style of percussion", he explained. The compositions were his own, those of a young artist of the world with eclectic tastes and not the traditional repertory.

Tulear, 1971. In a hut in the Mahavatse neighborhood, a group of kids armed with makeshift instruments, performed songs they had heard on the radio: French or American tunes, South African or Mozambican music, according to the waves that reached this forgotten land, in the extreme southwest of Madagascar. In a neighboring hut, there was a woman in a trance. Surrounded by relatives, she was prey to the caprices of the spirits which shook and transformed her. Suddenly she perceived the sound of an accordion behind the wall, and she was taken over by a frenzy of dancing (the accordion is an instrument of trance par excellence; in this region, accordionists are part of every ritual, of every celebration). Quickly, they sent for the providential musician. It turned out to be a twelve-year-old child, Regis Gizavo, who fled at the sight of the possessed woman. He was caught and brought back by force. He was forced tol play with his eyes closed, terrified, but would succeed little by little in calming the spirits and freeing the woman. The ambivalence of R?gis Gizavo's talent is entirely shown in this anecdote.

The son of a teacher with modern ideas who played the accordionist musette and taught it to five of his thirteen children, R?gis pursued management studies at the university, and played all kinds of music on his island and in Europe, where he has lived since 1990. But in his ethnic group Vezo (fishermen of the southwest coast of Madagascar), and all those which inhabit the Tulear region (Masikoro, Mahafaly...), the accordion has a religious connotation far too strong for R?gis not to have somehow been permeated by it. Every summer, he returned to his mother's village, Tampolo, on the other side of the Mangoky river where he listened to traditional accordionists; and even if he didn't learn the trance music, he grew up in their vibrations; their driving grooves emanate naturally forth from his fingers.

His first band was the Filibustiers, a group that entertains at local soirees. When he left the group to return to his studies, he was barely fifteen. After that he was hired by a more professional group, the Sailors, who accompanied the singer Angeline in concert and on the radio. The accordion belonged to the boss, as is often the case in Madagascar; R?gis didn't get his own instrument until 1990. At age twenty-five, after he graduated, he undertook a journey across the island which gave him the opportunity to play with numerous traditional! and modern musicians. Beginning in 1989, he started to record his own compositions with Landy, a singer from Tulear living in Tananarivo.

He also founded the group Jih? with the guitarist D?Gary, whom he knew from Tul?ar. In 1990, R?gis was the winner of the "D?couvertes" (Discoveries) musical competition organized by Radio France Internationale. He left for Europe where the music scene greeted him with a warm welcome and he was encouraged to pursue an international career. The drummer Francis Lassus invited him to join Bohe Combo, the group he was putting together. Regis accompanied Graeme Allwright, played on the albums of Zao, Higelin, les T?tes Bru!?es, etc.. and occasionally joined up with his old friend D'Gary, and the group Jih?. In 1993, he became the regular accordionist for I Muvrini, replacing the jazz musician Daniel Mille. The 330-odd concerts and sessions given in the span of two years at their side didn't stop him from working on his first solo album, which he recorded around Christmas 1995.
